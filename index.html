About Me

The background above is a photo I took during a few weeks of hiking and snowboarding with friends near the Cordón Caulle volcanic eruption that took place three hours drive from my hometown in Chile. I've spent about half my life in that part of the world, though that particular mountain trip took place in July 2011, in the course of a few years working on micro-finance after getting my economics degree.

The TL;DR of what's transpired in the years since: I moved to Chicago to experience living in a large U.S. city while working at a non-profit (the YMCA) in 2014, eventually joining and graduating from a web development boot camp (DBC) in 2016 before moving to Washington, D.C. to work at a rapidly growing tech company (Optoro) that same year, and then coming back to Chicago to continue my software engineering journey at DialogTech in early 2020.

While I initially studied economics to better understand human well-being, most of the work I've done has involved some aspect of business operations and information technology. I regularly explored new tech tools, and enjoyed dabbling in programming. After a few years of doing non-profit work, through software engineer friends I met while doing volunteer work, I decided to pursue programming as a career and joined a web development boot camp, DBC, in 2016.

DBC was a challenging, full-time, immersive web development program for dedicated beginners, focused on learning to code and shipping web applications. The coursework included Ruby/Rails, SQL, HTML/CSS, Javascript, domain modeling, "RESTful" design, consuming and producing APIs, git workflow, web deployment, as well as a strong focus on agile and test-driven development. In addition to growing my programming skills, in DBC I strengthened collaborative abilities like communication, empathy, and effective feedback to foster optimal teamwork environments both in and beyond the program.

A few months after graduating I found myself in my first post-boot-camp job in Washington, DC at a rapidly growing tech company, Optoro — where I got to be part of that company's environmentalist mission of eliminating waste from the reverse supply chain. I worked on the development of warehouse tools to aid clients in handling returned items in a more automated, data-driven way, resulting in said items finding their next best home. While I strengthened my programming skills, I was also part of challenging projects like improving our product's site reliability via implementing a more effective postmortem process, streamlining business to tech team issue resolution, to playing a not insignificant role in building out new teams.

In so far as the tech stack I focused on while working as a software engineer at Optoro, I programmed within a monolithic repo as well as a services ecosystem mostly in Ruby, Rails, Kotlin, Bash, SQL, Haml, Javascript, AngularJS, VueJS, and the necessary ops tools to deploy and monitor the products I worked on. Being in an agile team focused on delivering timely results, I shifted my focus to alleviating whatever bottlenecks we encountered — unblocking teammates via pair programming, resolving high impact outages while being on-call, flagging process issues as we worked to improve our teams, performing quality engineering duties, or whatever was needed to get the job done in a timely, cost-effective way.

My second (and current) software engineering role at DialogTech has been more heavily focused on the backend of the voice interfaces our clients use for voice tracking and analytics, no longer merely working with HTTP, but also SIP, using such tools as JRuby, PHP, Python, Bash, Ansible, and SQL. Given our company is undergoing a PCI certification process, I've become more familiar with that standard and its secure coding requirements. I've also gotten more acquainted with benchmarking and performance testing any changes to ensure they'll meet production demands, as SIP traffic is a lot less forgiving on the packet loss end (source).
